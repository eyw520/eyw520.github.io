Voices

June 23, 20226 min read

In the Fall of my frosh year, as I began diving into the vibrant, wonderful community that is Stanford, I began a small project called Voices. Largely inspired by the Humans of New York project, the idea behind it was simple, as was the blurb that I wrote when I first introduced it. It goes as follows:

We see many faces throughout our day. But just how many of those do we truly see? Voices aims to bridge the gap between the familiar and the foreign; between those we know and those we do not yet know. Because within every one of us is a story waiting to be set free.

It was an ambitious project. But, like many of my other projects, Voices ultimately came to an end after a short but fiery effort. While it lasted, however, I had the pleasure of collecting four short interviews, narratives shared by new friends I met.

————————————————

“I was a TA for a year, and I think that it was one of the experiences I’m really glad to have, to lead a class and teach other students. It allows you to learn the material a lot better, and it’s also a really unique experience that’s hard to get if you never think about being a TA. You spend a lot of time with your students. I had four three-hour sections, so I spent at least twelve hours in the lab to stay after and help. I didn’t like grading the tests — that was not my favorite. But I did like holding office hours and seeing the progress of my students. It’s a very rewarding feeling. I think having a good TA can really enhance your learning experience in a class.”

John majored in Electrical Engineering and Computer Science and will be working at Google this coming fall. He will return to Stanford to work on a few remaining projects.

(Tresidder Memorial Union)

————————————————

“It’d be really nice to have some more motivation beyond “oh, that’s cool, I want to learn more about it”. Or just finally knowing what direction I want to go with my life. When I was applying to college, a lot of people said ‘oh, I want to dedicate my life to researching this disease’ or ‘oh, I want to help humanity’. I can respect that, but I probably would not be okay with doing that unless I genuinely thought researching that disease was interesting. I’d just like to have some greater motivation or direction in life. I guess that’s just the college existential angst. I think I’m fine with not knowing, but it’d be nice to know. Totally not contradictory.”

Nathan is a frosh living in Sally Ride this year. Originally from Indiana, he is currently intent on studying Computer Science and Mathematics during his time at Stanford.

(Stern, Sally Ride)

————————————————

I miss my twin sister. That’s pretty rough. Recently, she cut her hair super short, and it really got me the other night because I realized that her hair is growing and I’m not there to see it grow. And that just makes me really sad. There’s this one Saturday night where I went out to talk to my other friend, but then she talked about my sister, so I ended up crying in the hammock for two hours. She’s on the semester system, so the last time I saw her was this past August.

There was one weekend where we spontaneously went to two concerts in a row, then we went to another concert the week after. The first concert was the most spontaneous because we bought the tickets the night below to see Shawn Mendes. I love Shawn Mendes — he was at the Hollywood Bowl, so we went there for fifteen dollars. We were in the back, so we were just listening to other people and talking and hanging out. I wanted to film some of Shawn Mendes, so my sister filmed it for me just so I could appreciate the moment. That was really nice.

Originally from Los Angeles, Julie is a freshman living at Sally Ride, Stern. She has recently learned to eat ice cream with chopsticks, an impressive feat by many standards.

(The Axe and Palm)

————————————————

I’m here with my boyfriend and he’s busy and he’s studying a lot and that means I’m alone a lot of the time. The community makes me feel connected, though. Everybody is so close, and you can do anything. It never feels dangerous, so it feels good to be here.

There’s a lot that’s great here. You can go to the gym. You have a meal plan. You can go to the pool. The libraries are great. You don’t need a car; you can just bike everywhere. I’m from Iceland, so I need to drive 10 minutes just to get anywhere. It’s all just really good — it’s hard to put anything first.

Eva is currently staying with her boyfriend on campus. Originally from Iceland, she hopes to study graphic design at Stanford this coming year.

(Meyer Green)
