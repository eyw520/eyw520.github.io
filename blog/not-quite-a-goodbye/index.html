Not Quite a Goodbye

July 3, 20235 min read

A post-graduation update, first shared via email through my mailing list, Timshel. A brief retrospective on senior year, and initial answers to the question: what’s next?

Dear friend,

Just over two weeks ago, I graduated from Stanford with a B.S. degree in Computer Science and a minor in Mathematics. Walking at commencement marked the end to my undergraduate career, the end of four years of this fever dream called Stanford. And though I won’t be saying goodbye for good, things will change substantially this coming year. This Summer, I’ll be working full time at Hippocratic AI, at our office right off campus (just by Verve coffee in Palo Alto, come say hi!). In the Fall, I’ll be studying part-time at Stanford, living in Munger and finishing up the remainder of my Master’s degree.

Summer brings with it a brief reprieve. Without classes, it’s been much easier to find a semblance of work-life balance, even with the new responsibilities I’m picking up at work. I’m in the office from just before 9 AM to just after 6 PM. I spend my evenings working out in the gym, writing journals and blog posts, and ramping up on projects archived from seasons ago. It’s quiet on campus. I am surrounded by a silence I didn’t know I needed, and it’s a welcome change from the deafening roar of normal campus life.

So, what’s next?

In the short term, not too much. I’ll just be working and doing my best to find balance during this transitional period. I’m reclaiming time from my day and finding ways to incorporate more intentional learning into my work. I’m discovering the limits of where my work-life boundaries will lie long term. I’ve been splitting my time between Stanford and San Francisco. If you’re around either of those areas, shoot me a message, and we’ll find time to hang out.

In the long term, there’s a lot to look forward to.

On the professional front, there’s plenty of learning on the horizon. I am constantly growing, not only as an engineer, but as a leader and future entrepreneur. I’ll make the most of my time at Hippocratic, but I refuse to get too complacent. I’m constantly looking for opportunities as new innovations come.

On the artistic front, I’m looking into creating short-form content, hopefully combining my interests in writing, video editing, storytelling, and machine learning to explore new mediums for expression. I’m looking to bring my work to a wider audience, and to iterate as feedback comes.

On the personal front, I’m hoping to build habits for the life ahead. I hope to be a better friend, to be more intentional about reaching out and strengthening friendships cultivated over these past four years. If you’re reading this: hold me to my word.

Onto other news. I’m sunsetting the meal Calendly and polaroid wall. Thank you to everybody who humored me and booked a meal, or gave me five minutes of your time for a polaroid. It truly meant a lot to me. Time flies; so much of senior year breezed by before me, the time escaping from my grasp. but looking back at the two stacks of polaroids on my desk, neatly stacked and secured by three rubber bands each, I can recall the happiness each of you brought me and laugh at the creative license some of you took in your doodles. Thank you, truly.

Keep an eye out on the blog. It’ll see some updates in the next two months. New posts, projects, and reflections are coming. Summer is usually a great time for writing.

I’ll end my letter here. It’s the evening of July 4th, and Kaitlin and I just had dinner at Lakeside. The night is young, and the cool summer night is inviting. Perhaps I’ll take a walk around the now-marshy lake or climb MemAud at sunset. For the first time in a while, I find myself at the helm, the world laid out before me. In front of me, a blank canvas, clear as freshly fallen snow. What will I choose to draw?

With love,

Eden
